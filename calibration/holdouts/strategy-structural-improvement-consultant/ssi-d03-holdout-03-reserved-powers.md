# Reserved Powers Matrix

The reserved powers matrix needs to make authority visible enough that neither the board nor management has to guess during a redesign. It should cover capital, physician compensation, service changes, and any decision that changes risk or control.

## Matrix
| Decision Domain | Management Authority | Required Approval | Threshold | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Capital purchase above $250,000 | Management may recommend | Board finance committee | $250,000 | Material capital risk must stay visible |
| Physician compensation plan | Management drafts | Board or compensation committee | Any new plan | Referral and FMV risk |
| Launch or close a service line | Management analyzes | Board approval | Any material change | Changes access and strategy |
| Debt issuance | Management cannot finalize alone | Full board | All debt | Balance-sheet risk is reserved |
| Charity policy revision | Management drafts | Board quality or audit committee | Any revision | Community benefit and IRS risk |
| IT architecture change | CIO may approve within budget | Board only if enterprise-wide risk | Architecture shifts | Cyber and workflow impact |

## Governance Implications
- Reserve powers should be short, explicit, and limited to decisions that truly move risk or control.
- Delegation should be broad enough for speed but narrow enough that the board can still demonstrate oversight.
- The matrix should be reviewed with legal and compliance before it is rolled out.

The matrix makes decision rights explicit so governance does not depend on memory or politics during a reorganization. The board retains the decisions that change risk, capital, and control; management keeps the operational calls that need speed and local context.
